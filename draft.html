We are back from the na... traverse. Its been a tough six days, and now we are completely shattered and drinking lots of water. Also everything stinks. I have this fear we'll be kicked out the out of the hostel due to the smell. Hers some notes on the route, which might be of use to others.

Maps and stuff: we used the lonely planet book trekking in the patagonian andes. The times in the book are optimistic. Speculation as to why:

  • The Author retired from the SAS, or some other job that required running up hills.
  • The Author wasn't carrying a test, food, stove, etc.
  • The author was in the process of writing a book on patagonian treks and was therefore somewhat fitter than us
  • The author had spent the past few years working as a mountain guide

I like the first theory, though the last two have the advantage of being true.

The map was kinda crappy too. Crags aren't marked. I suppose that if you want a map with crags they start with a black bit of paper.

The nag was pretty easy though the trip, but mostly because we could see the way owning to good weather. It would be a pain to do in poor weather.

We drank the water from the rivers. It was lovely and freezing cold. We often had our cups on the outside of our bags, and dipped them into streams. We pasted a gang of scouts singing the spanish version of "I want to go awandering". Thats our life now. drinking water from streams. signing kids. green trees. Lewis and Clark wandering past. foxes barking.

In less good water news, the folks down in CAB suggested added a drop of bleach to each liter of water.

The book claims you can get food, sleep etc in huts known as refiocos. we shunned this options, mostly because we had the tent etc, anwe didnÂ´t fancy booking ahread. One hears mixed reports about the quality of the palces, how watertight they are etc.

Day One

We headed up the ski lift (this is the primary route in the book) mostly due to the parties of Argentine teenagers heading for the path up to Frey. We were warned about the wind at the bottom. It was a cold strong wind, but we were pretty stable on our feet when we were up there. We did end up wearing thick gloves, balaclava, many layers etc. Only one lift at the ski centre was operating, so we took that one and had to figure out where we were at the top.

We did the traverse from the ridge above the ski center to the turning for refoico frey This is mostly over shattered rock, following the dots. There was good views over the ridge at a low point. The turn off to frey (the left) and (Jacob the right) was drawn on a rock.

The book suggests dropping down to Frey, then climbing back up and down the other side the next day. We shunned this option. It featured a pile more height gain and loss, Megan (Who we had met while white water rafting) desrbied her nightr there as a effort to hold the tent down in the wind, and we didn't fancy hanging around with tons of teenagers.

Its worth noting that the trek had quite a few parties of folk in different levels of gear. We saw folk in trainers, without rain prtection, etc more here.

slow progress down to the vally - long scree desent. Camped in the Vally- its a big site along the path. We didn't see anyone else that night, but more folks showed up when we went to bed. In the evening, three dogs came and sniffed around. No idea how they got into the park. It was pretty cold in the valley.
